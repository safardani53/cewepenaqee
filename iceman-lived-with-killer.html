We had no idea our church-going dad was the 'iceman' killer who murdered 200 people over 30 years, s

Posted by Aldo Pusey on Wednesday, May 1, 2024

TO the outside world, Richard Kuklinski was the all-American dad who cooked BBQs for his neighbours, took his family on annual trips to Disneyworld and attended church each Sunday.

But behind the smokescreen was a serial killer and professional hitman who shot victims in the Adam's apple and fed others to rats.

A new documentary, I Lived With A Killer, shines a light on the monster who admitted killing more than 200 people over 30 years before he was arrested in 1986.

He was known as the 'iceman' because he froze his victims' bodies to conceal their time of death.

But interviews with his daughters, Merrick and Christin, reveal that although they knew he had a violent streak, they had no idea he was a twisted killer.

At one stage, they plotted to kill their terrifying dad in a bid to stop his violence against their mother but were too scared to go through with it.

Killed first victim at 14

Kuklinski, who was born to a violent alcoholic father and religious mother, developed his thirst for blood at a very young age.

He beat a local bully to death with a pole and buried him in the woods, when he was just 14.

Then over the next 10 years, he became a professional hitman for the mafia - namely the DeCavalcante crime family in Newark - and murdered people on the streets just for extracurricular practice. 

No method was off limits and Kuklinski used needles, guns, hunting knives, poison, rope and a bow and arrow to kill.

His favourite technique, he once said, was a nasal spray bottle filled with cyanide.

But he denied ever using chainsaws because “that would leave pieces of meat all over my shirt, why would I want to ruin a good shirt?” 

Victim was eaten alive by rats - and he filmed every second

Kuklinski met his wife Barbara Pedrici when he was 25 and she was 18. He was charming and courteous, often presenting her with flowers when he picked her up from work. 

But as the months passed, Barbara gradually realised she had become isolated from her friends and family and he regularly beat her. 

When she told him she wanted to end their year-long relationship, he stabbed her with a hunting knife so sharp she didn't even feel the blade go in.

Barbara survived the attack and was so terrified that Kuklinski would kill her if she left, she married him.

Unbeknown to her, by the time they were married in 1961, Kuklinski had already committed 65 murders, sometimes for fun but more often than not for money to fund his taste for expensive cars and designer clothes.

In 1972, Kuklinski was offered $10,000 (about £100,000 in today's money) to kill a man - and double if he made him suffer. 

The financial incentive pushed him to a new level of twisted creativity.

He flagged his victim down at a stop sign and put a gun to his head before bundling him into the trunk of his car.

Kuklinski took the man to a cave in Pennsylvania where he rigged up camera equipment and left him to be eaten alive by rats. 

Four days later he returned to find disjointed bones of the man and a camera reel documenting his slow and painful death.

On another occasion Kuklinski shot his victim in his Adam's apple after making a bet about how long it would take for him to die.

It took a few minutes for him to drown in his own blood, longer than the killer had predicted - leaving him £40 out of pocket. 

I wanted to kill my father to protect my mother

Kuklinski's violent nature continued at home.

His family didn't know about his murderous behaviour but he frequently held his wife around the neck, throttling her in front of the children.

With nowhere to go, Kuklinski's youngest daughter Christin came up with a plan.

“My mother and I would talk about how we could kill my father because it was the only way we could possibly be safe," she says on the documentary.

“It would have to be a guarantee because we would never have a second chance.”

Christin and her mother conjured up a plan to drug his meatloaf with Valium but they became too terrified to put their plan into action. 

“He would hit himself in the head so hard you could see the blood vessels popping," Christin said when describing his rage.

“We had no hope in being saved because the police had been tracking him for so long and they couldn’t get him on anything. So how were we supposed to stop him." 

His eldest daughter, Merrick, had an unbreakable bond with her father, in spite of his violent behaviour. 

After many failed undercover police attempts to catch Kuklinski, he was finally arrested in 1986. Kuklinski was convicted and given two life sentences.

Christin said: “Jail was perfect for my father. It was where he could be the king and he was very well respected. He got to hang out with the other gangsters in prison and play cards.”

Kuklinski eventually died in prison after 18 years behind bars. Before his death, he admitted to killing over 200 people and said he never felt a pang of remorse or guilt. 

I Lived With A Killer: The Iceman will air at 9pm tonight on Crime+Investigation.
